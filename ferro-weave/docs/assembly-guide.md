# Assembly Guide — Ferro Weave

This guide walks through building a Ferro Weave board from a bare PCB to
a working B-H loop tracer. Estimated time: **4–6 hours** for someone
comfortable with SMD soldering.

## 1. What you need

- Ferro Weave PCB (4-layer, 80×120 mm — gerbers in `schematic/`)
- All parts from [`hardware/BOM.csv`](../hardware/BOM.csv)
- Hot-air rework station or reflow oven (the LM5035 / IRFH7446 / QFN
  parts need hot-air; the rest can be hand-soldered with a fine iron)
- 0.5 mm solder paste + stencil
- Multimeter, bench supply (0–5 V / 2 A), ST-Link v2, USB-C cable
- microSD card (any size, FAT32-formatted)
- A toroidal specimen + enamelled copper wire for the windings

## 2. PCB layout notes

The PCB is 4-layer with a stackup tuned for mixed-signal work:

| Layer | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Top   | analog front end (integrator, sense), signal routing |
| Inner 1 | solid GND plane (analog + digital, split under the integrator) |
| Inner 2 | power plane (VBAT, +15V, −15V islands) |
| Bottom | ESP32-C3 module, STM32, digital, SD, OLED connector |

The power-stage (LM5035 + FETs + boost) is in a fenced-off corner with
its own GND return so switching noise doesn't couple into the
integrator. Keep the integrator cap leads **as short as possible** —
10 mm or less — to keep leakage and stray capacitance low.

## 3. Populate in this order

1. **Power section** — TP4056, MAX17048, AMS1117-3.3, TPS61040 boost,
   inverting charge-pump diodes + caps, LM5035 + 2× IRFH7446.
2. **Analog front end** — OPA2188 integrator, TS5A3166 reset switch,
   100 nF C0G integrator cap, 0.1 Ω sense resistor, NTC, Rsense divider.
3. **Digital** — STM32G474 (LQFP64, hot-air), ESP32-C3-MINI-1 module.
4. **Connectors** — USB-C, microSD socket, 4 mm banana posts,
   SSD1306 OLED (soldered or on a 0.5 mm pitch FFC).
5. **Passives** — all resistors, caps, LEDs, buttons.

## 4. The ±15 V rail

The TPS61040 boosts 3.7 V → +15 V at up to 1 A. The −15 V rail is
generated by a charge-pump inverter hung off the boost switch node
(two B140 Schottky diodes + two 4.7 µF caps). This gives enough headroom
for the OPA2188 (rail-to-rail output at ±15 V → ±14 V usable) and the
LM5035 high-side bootstrap. See the schematic sheet "Power" for the
exact topology.

## 5. Wind the specimen

1. Use `scripts/wind_calc.py` to compute N1, N2, l_e, A_core for your
   toroid geometry.
2. Wind N1 primary turns evenly around the toroid (enamelled Cu, 0.4 mm
   for ~1 A).
3. Wind N2 secondary turns on top of (or next to) the primary.
4. Strip and tin the leads; connect to the red/black banana posts:
   - **Primary** → red+/black- pair (labelled "P")
   - **Secondary** → red+/black- pair (labelled "S")

A 3D-printable winding jig STL is provided in `docs/winding_jig.stl`
(renders a toroid holder with a turn counter).

## 6. Flash firmware

- **STM32** over SWD (ST-Link):
  ```bash
  openocd -f interface/stlink.cfg -f target/stm32g4x.cfg \
          -c "program build-stm32/ferro_weave.elf verify reset exit"
  ```
- **ESP32-C3** over USB-C:
  ```bash
  cd firmware/esp32
  idf.py -p /dev/ttyACM0 flash monitor
  ```

## 7. First bring-up

1. **No specimen connected.** Plug USB-C to charge the LiPo (red LED).
2. Once charged (green LED), press **SWEEP** — the OLED should show
   "FAULT / no load" (the OCP trips because the primary is open). This
   confirms the protection path works.
3. Connect a 10 Ω power resistor across the primary posts (no
   secondary). Press **SWEEP** — you should see a thin straight-line
   "loop" (pure resistor, no hysteresis) on the OLED.
4. Wind a known specimen (e.g. a 3E25 ferrite toroid) and sweep it.
   Compare the result to the datasheet values; see
   [`calibration-guide.md`](calibration-guide.md).

## 8. Mechanical

The board fits in a 3D-printed enclosure (STL in `docs/enclosure.stl`)
with cutouts for the OLED, USB-C, banana posts, and the three buttons.
The power-stage FETs dissipate up to ~1.5 W during a ±2 A sweep —
ventilation slots on the enclosure bottom are required.
